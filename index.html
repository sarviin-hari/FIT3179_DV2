From 2019 to 2023, Selangor, Sabah, and Sarawak had the largest working-age populations, with Selangor leading in employment, reaching 3.8 million. Smaller states like Perlis and Labuan had lower employment. Unemployment remained stable, though Sabah saw peaks during the COVID-19 pandemic, with 165,600 unemployed in 2022. ] A significant number remained outside the labour force, especially in Selangor and Sabah, with over 1.2 million and 900,000, respectively.

Male workforce dominates, but female participation is almost equal for Services

The chart shows gender distribution across sectors. In Agriculture, males slightly outnumber females, but the gap is smaller than in other sectors. Mining and Quarrying is heavily male-dominated, with minimal female presence. Manufacturing and Construction also have fewer females. The Services sector shows a more balanced gender ratio, though males still dominate.

Women are more likely to be out of the workforce due to "Family Duty" and "School", especially in "Family Duty", where females outnumber males, indicating a potential barrier to participation.

Unemployment

Work Experience of Unemployed Individuals by Age Group

The visualization shows that among younger individuals (15-24), most unemployed have never worked. In middle age groups (25-39), the gap narrows, while in older groups (40-64), most have worked before, indicating younger individuals are more likely to have never worked.

The visualization highlights changes in unemployment duration in Malaysia from 2017 to 2023. The less than 3 months category rose sharply, reaching 68% in 2023, showing faster job placements. The 3 to 6 months category dropped from 30% to 18% in 2023, indicating shorter unemployment periods. The 6 months to 1 year category remained stable at 9-14%, with a slight dip to 9% in 2023.

Long-term unemployment, over 1 year, fell steadily to just 5% in 2023, suggesting better job market conditions.

The chart shows that from 2017 to 2023, School and Family Responsibility consistently led as reasons for non-participation, with Family Responsibility briefly overtaking School in 2020. Retirement remains steady in third place, while Further Studies and Disabled fluctuate. Not Interested ranks the lowest. Overall, non-participation is mainly driven by education and family obligations.

The chart shows the gender distribution of non-participation in the labor force, highlighting key trends. Family Duty significantly affects females with 2,984.8 compared to 85.2 for males. The School category has 1,298.7 males and 1,459.3 females, showing a slightly higher impact on females.

Retired values are higher for males at 498.2, while Disabled shows 183.4 males and 70.1 females. In Further Study, males report 60.1 versus 54 for females, and No Interest has 62.8 males versus 15.4 females. The chart reveals clear gender disparities in non-participation, with Family Duty and School being major factors for women.
